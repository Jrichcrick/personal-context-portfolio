# Marathon Nutrition & Recovery Plan

**Athlete:** JR  
**Goal:** Sub-4:00 NYC Marathon, November 1, 2026  
**Age context:** Recovery matters more than it used to. Budget for it.

---

## Pre-Run Nutrition (Day Before and Morning Of)

### What works for you specifically
- **Lower fiber before runs** — you feel heavier and sluggish if fiber is high
- **More protein/meat the day before** — you feel better and run better
- This is your gut telling you something real. Honor it every time.

### Day Before a Long Run or Quality Session

| Meal | What to eat | What to avoid |
|------|-------------|---------------|
| Breakfast | Eggs, bacon or sausage, white toast | Oatmeal, bran, raw vegetables |
| Lunch | Chicken/beef + white rice or pasta, small salad | Large salads, legumes, whole grains |
| Dinner | Steak or chicken + white pasta or rice, light sauce | Broccoli, beans, lentils, heavy sauces |
| Snacks | Banana, white bread with peanut butter | High-fiber bars, raw fruit with skin |

**Hydrate more than usual** — aim for pale yellow urine by end of day.

### Race Morning (applies to long runs too)

- **2–3 hours before:** 400–600 calories. Bagel + peanut butter + banana. Keep it familiar — don't experiment.
- **60 min before:** Small snack if needed (half a banana, a few crackers)
- **Coffee:** Fine if you drink it normally. Don't add it race morning if you don't usually drink it.
- **No new foods race morning.** Ever.

## Fueling During Runs

### When to fuel
- **Under 60 minutes:** Water only
- **60–90 minutes:** Optional gel or electrolytes at the end
- **Over 90 minutes:** Fuel consistently — don't wait until you feel low

### Long Run Fueling Protocol
- First gel at **mile 5** (roughly 45–50 min in)
- Then every **40–45 minutes** after that
- Wash down with water, not Gatorade (prevents GI upset)
- Electrolytes (salt tabs or Gatorade) every 45–60 min in warm weather

### Race Day Fueling
- Gel at miles **5, 10, 15, 20**
- Drink water at every aid station (small sip — no gulping)
- Gatorade at every other aid station, or per thirst
- **Only use gels/drinks you've trained with.** NYC provides Gatorade Endurance — train with it or carry your own.

### Gel Options
Pick one and stick with it all training. Your stomach needs to learn it.
- Gu Energy (most common, easy to find)
- Maurten (low-osmolality, gentler on stomach — worth trying if you've had GI issues)
- Spring Energy (real food, works well for some)

## Post-Run Nutrition

### Within 30–45 minutes of finishing
This window matters most for recovery, especially as you get older.

- **Protein + carbs:** 20–30g protein, 40–60g carbs
- Examples: chocolate milk (genuinely ideal), Greek yogurt + banana, eggs + toast, protein shake + fruit
- Don't skip this because you're not hungry. It directly affects how you feel the next day.

### Dinner after long runs
- Higher protein than normal (6–8oz meat)
- Moderate carbs to replenish glycogen
- Don't overfill — you're recovering, not rewarding

### Hydration after long runs
- Weigh yourself before and after if you want to dial this in (each pound lost = ~16oz to replenish)
- Minimum: drink consistently for the next 4–6 hours, targeting pale yellow urine

| Day | Priority |
|-----|----------|
| Monday (boot camp) | Normal eating, slightly higher protein for muscle repair. Weeks 7+: low fiber dinner — Tuesday shakeout is next morning |
| Tuesday (rest → shakeout wk 7+) | Weeks 1–6: normal day, no prep needed. Weeks 7+: low fiber Monday dinner already done |
| Wednesday (quality) | **Pre-run protocol Tuesday dinner** — this is your hard day, every week |

| Friday (always a run day) | Easy weeks 1–6, medium-long weeks 7+. Thursday dinner sets this up |
| Saturday (rest) | Pre-long-run day — follow full protocol for Sunday |
| Sunday (long run) | Saturday dinner + race morning protocol |

## Sleep

### Why it matters more now
Sleep is when your body actually builds the fitness you earned in training. Skimping on sleep doesn't just make you tired — it literally reduces the adaptation. For older athletes this effect is stronger.

### Targets
- **Regular nights:** 7.5–8 hours minimum
- **Night before long runs:** 8–9 hours if possible
- **Peak training weeks (weeks 9–16):** Prioritize sleep over everything non-essential

### Practical tips
- **Consistent wake time** matters more than bedtime — it anchors your rhythm
- **No alcohol within 3 hours of sleep** — it destroys sleep quality even if it helps you fall asleep
- **No hard training within 4 hours of bed** — elevated cortisol keeps you awake
- **Cool room (65–68°F)** — sleep quality drops in warm rooms
- **Dark room** — use blackout shades or a sleep mask if needed
- **If you slept badly before a long run:** Do it anyway, but back off pace. One bad night won't ruin the run. A string of them will.

### Race week sleep
Don't stress if you sleep badly the night before the race — almost everyone does. The night *two nights before* (Friday Oct 30) is the one that actually matters. Protect that one.

## Stretching

### Never static stretch cold muscles
Do it after runs, not before.

### Before runs: Dynamic warm-up (5 minutes)
- Leg swings (front-back and side-to-side): 10 each leg
- Hip circles: 10 each direction
- High knees: 20 reps
- Butt kicks: 20 reps
- Lateral shuffles: 10 yards each way
- Easy jog: first mile always slower than easy pace

### After runs: Static stretching (10 minutes minimum)
Hold each stretch 30–45 seconds. Don't rush.

| Stretch | Targets |
|---------|---------|
| Standing quad pull | Quads |
| Figure-4 (standing or lying) | Glutes, piriformis |
| Runner's lunge | Hip flexors |
| Seated hamstring reach | Hamstrings |
| Calf stretch (wall) | Calves, Achilles |
| IT band cross-stretch (standing) | IT band |
| Doorway chest opener | Chest, shoulders (for posture) |

### After long runs: Add foam rolling (10–15 minutes)
- IT band — slow, painful, necessary
- Quads — front and sides
- Calves — especially around Achilles insertion
- Upper back / thoracic spine

## Cross-Training

### Why it matters for you
You can't just run forever and skip this part anymore. Cross-training:
- Builds aerobic fitness without pounding joints
- Fixes muscle imbalances running creates
- Keeps you sane during taper
- Substitutes for a run if you feel injury brewing

### What works best for marathon training

| Activity | Benefit | When to use |
|----------|---------|-------------|
| Cycling (bike or spin) | Aerobic base, quad + glute strength, zero impact | Any rest day, or swap for easy runs when legs are beat |
| Swimming | Full body, zero impact, great for mental reset | Rest days, injury prevention |
| Yoga | Mobility, hip flexors, breathing, stress | 1–2x/week, especially during peak phase |
| Strength training | Injury prevention, power, running economy | 2x/week on rest days |

### Strength Training (2x/week, 20–30 min)

These are the moves that directly reduce injury risk for marathon runners:

**Lower body / hips:**
- Single-leg squats (or Bulgarian split squats): 3×10 each leg
- Glute bridges or hip thrusts: 3×15
- Clamshells with band: 3×20 each side
- Calf raises (single-leg, slow): 3×15 each

**Core:**
- Dead bug: 3×10 each side
- Bird-dog: 3×10 each side
- Plank: 3×30–45 sec
- Side plank: 3×20–30 sec each side

**Posture / upper body:**
- Rows (band or cable): 3×12
- Shoulder press: 3×10

Do this on Monday and Wednesday (rest days). Takes 25 minutes.

## Managing the Hard Days

### Why you feel worse the day after a hard run
Completely normal. Hard efforts create micro-damage in muscle fibers. The next day your body is repairing and inflamed. Going hard again before repairs complete = compounding fatigue and injury risk. The plan is built around this — every hard day has an easy or rest day after it.

### Signs you should flip a hard day to easy
- Legs still feel heavy from the last hard session
- You slept poorly 2+ nights in a row
- Resting heart rate is elevated by 5+ bpm above normal
- You feel "off" in a way you can't explain

**When in doubt, go easy.** Missing one quality session hurts less than getting injured.

### Signs you should take an unplanned rest day
- Sharp or one-sided pain (especially knee, shin, or foot)
- Any heel or Achilles pain in the morning
- Fever or illness
- Extreme fatigue after normal sleep

## Race Week Specifics

- **No new foods the week of the race.** Not the restaurant near the expo, not the pasta dinner the night before.
- **Carb load starting Wednesday Oct 28:** 70–75% of calories from carbs, cut fat and fiber
- **Salt more than usual** the last 2–3 days to help water retention and prevent hyponatremia
- **Avoid alcohol** after Wednesday Oct 28
- **Friday Oct 31:** Full pre-run protocol. Eat early, sleep early.
- **Race morning:** Eat 2.5–3 hours before your corral time. NYC start times are early — figure out your exact gun time and work backwards.
